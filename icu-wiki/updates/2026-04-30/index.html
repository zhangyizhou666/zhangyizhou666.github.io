Weekly Update

We compared the initial 50-question Gemma/Qwen benchmark with the harder Qwen3.5 20-question benchmark and clarified the next evaluation step.

evaluationbenchmark

Summary

This week focused on evaluating whether our current RAG benchmark is strong enough to separate small models in a meaningful way.

  • We preserved the earlier 50-question Gemma/Qwen evaluation from last week's seminar as a historical baseline.
  • We found that the initial 50-question benchmark produced similar metric-level RAGAS scores across model sizes, which suggested that the questions were too easy or too generic.
  • We evaluated Qwen3.5 0.8B, 2B, and 4B on a harder 20-question benchmark designed to better reflect real student questions.
  • We added saved answer examples so the score plots can be interpreted together with real model behavior.

Initial 50-question Baseline

Before the hard-question benchmark, we evaluated Gemma 3 and Qwen3.5 models on 50 broad ICU questions. The Gemma answers were generated through OpenRouter API calls, while the Qwen3.5 answers were generated locally.

The useful takeaway was not a strong model-size conclusion. The metric-level RAGAS scores were relatively close, which suggested that the original questions were too easy and too generic to reveal practical chatbot failures.

Qwen3.5 Hard-question Baseline

We evaluated Qwen3.5 0.8B, 2B, and 4B on a 20-question hard realistic benchmark using the same retrieval cache.

The current result does not show a clean elbow point. The 4B model is stronger on answer correctness, but the smaller models are not separated enough across every metric to claim a simple linear model-size relationship.

Interpretation

The current evidence supports a cautious thesis direction:

  • The hard-question benchmark is more useful than the earlier simple questions because it exposes weaknesses that the original evaluation did not capture clearly.
  • RAGAS scores alone are not enough; manual review and human evaluation are still needed.
  • Answerability is teacher-generated benchmark metadata, not a native RAGAS metric.
  • The strongest next step is to keep the retrieval pipeline fixed, improve question quality, and add a small human-evaluation layer.
